Showcase Francis’s ability to write warm, sensory, conversational promotional copy that sells an experience rather than simply listing what a business offers.

The page should feel lively and appetising without becoming visually busy.

We can’t help it.

## The Opening

**Section label**

Setting the table

**Featured excerpt**

> Imagine this: the best local and international dishes, prepared by master chefs, at a restaurant you can practically stroll to — or at your home, office, parties, and events.

**Follow-up**

> Well, you don’t really need to imagine it, because that’s exactly what Chef Marco Barrila and his team are doing at Insatiable Eats.

**Editorial note**

The opening begins with the experience before introducing the business.

It gives the reader the food, the convenience, and the possibility first. The brand arrives after there is already something to want.

## The Voice

**Large line**

> Hear me out.

**Body**

That small line says a lot about the tone of the piece.

The copy does not speak from a distance. It talks to the reader, moves quickly, and keeps the experience human.

That conversational energy runs through both versions of the work.

## Service Without the Service List

**Section label**

More than a restaurant

**Featured excerpt**

> Insatiable Eats gives you a customised dining experience, with options for private dining, renting the entire restaurant or part of it with premium catering for your parties and events, or bringing the catering to you.

**Body**

The business had several offers to communicate.

The writing connects them through one idea: the diner’s enjoyment and convenience.

That keeps the piece focused on what people get to experience rather than presenting a long list of services.

## Project Reflection

**Heading**

The work behind the appetite

**Body**

Food copy has an obvious job: make people want the food.

But this piece also had to make people understand the range of the business — restaurant dining, catering, private events, fresh pasta, and more.

The answer was to keep returning to the experience.

What can you taste?  
Where can you enjoy it?  
How easy is it to get?

The details changed. The appetite held everything together.

## Content Notes

- This page should not display both original copy versions in full as uninterrupted text.
- Use excerpts, oversized lines, typographic pacing, and short editorial transitions.
- The visual treatment should feel inspired by dining, menus, editorial food culture, or hospitality without becoming a literal restaurant website.
- Do not invent a client brief, campaign objective, target audience, process, results, or metrics.
- The phrases “the job was” and “needed to” describe what can reasonably be inferred from the supplied copy. They must not be presented as a quoted or documented client brief.
- The original work uses frequent ellipses and exclamation marks. The excerpts here are lightly edited for readability and portfolio presentation.
- Preserve Francis’s conversational energy while avoiding excessive punctuation.
- Verify unusual food terms and proper names before final publication.
- Do not use generic stock food imagery simply to fill space. Any imagery should materially support the editorial presentation.
- The final visual composition will be defined during the visual direction and storyboard phases.
