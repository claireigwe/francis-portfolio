# Online Shop Sales Copy

## Page Purpose

Showcase Francis’s ability to use humour, conversational language, and familiar observations to make a straightforward sales argument more memorable.

The page should feel quick, playful, and confident.

## Opening

**Headline**

Your products are the coolest things since Nike launched Jordans.

**Follow-up**

The problem is, only a few people know that right now.

**Introduction**

The offer was simple: online shops for sellers.

The writing could have started with features, years of experience, or the importance of digital transformation.

It did not.

It started with a compliment and immediately turned it into the problem the service needed to solve.

---

## The Problem

**Section label**

Good product. Small audience.

**Featured excerpt**

> You know your service is just what the market needs. The market just doesn’t know you yet.

**Large line**

> Blame it on not having an online shop.

**Body**

The argument does not make the seller the problem.

The product may already be good.

The service may already be useful.

The missing piece is access to the market.

That gives the sales copy a simple direction: make the gap visible, then show how the offer closes it.

---

## The Proof Point

**Section label**

Why trust the offer?

**Featured excerpt**

> We have been in the business of creating high-converting online shops for sellers since 2006.

**Supporting excerpt**

> Our clients have seen increased sales after trusting us to bring the world to their online doorsteps.

**Editorial note**

The copy moves briefly from personality to credibility.

The tone stays conversational, but the argument now has something practical behind it: experience and a claimed business outcome.

---

## Then Come the Grandparents

**Section label**

Making the market shift memorable

**Heading**

Even gran is online.

**Featured excerpt**

> The world is changing so fast that even gran is ordering her recipes online.

**Follow-up excerpt**

> With the current lockdown, even gramps has started ordering his socks on “The Amazon” while trying to chat you up on “The Facebook.”

**Large line**

> As dry as his jokes are, they also speak one truth: online is where the market is.

**Body**

The humour is doing more than decorating the copy.

It makes the shift to online shopping feel obvious, familiar, and difficult to ignore.

Instead of explaining digital adoption with statistics, the piece turns it into a family observation.

---

## The Sales Argument

**Section label**

Keeping the promise simple

**Featured excerpt**

> We know how to open that market up for sellers.

**Follow-up**

> All you have to do is have a good product or service. The rest is up to us.

**Body**

By this point, the piece has already established:

- the seller may have something worth buying;
- too few people know about it;
- the market has moved online;
- the service exists to help close that gap.

The final promise does not need to become complicated.

---

## The Close

**Section label**

One last push

**Large line**

> Click now to unleash your sales power.

**Editorial note**

The original close is louder than the rest of the piece.

That contrast reflects the direct-response style of the copy: conversation first, action last.

---

## Project Reflection

**Heading**

Humour with a job to do

**Body**

The strongest joke in sales copy is rarely the one that gets the biggest laugh.

It is the one that makes the point easier to remember.

In this piece, the humour supports a simple argument:

Your product may not be the problem. Visibility is.

The grandparents, “The Amazon,” and “The Facebook” all lead back to the same idea.

The market is online.

The service helps sellers get there.
