## Opening

**Headline**

Every company wants to improve.

**Follow-up**

Not every leader wants to go where the problems are.

**Featured excerpt**

> From Fortune 500 companies to single-employee outfits, every business brand wants to improve.

**Introduction**

The article begins with a familiar business obsession: improvement.

Lean management. Design thinking. Problem-solving frameworks. The Pareto principle.

Companies rarely lack ideas for getting better.

The argument Francis develops is that the problem may sit somewhere else entirely: leaders becoming disconnected from the place where the work actually happens.

---

## The Central Idea

**Section label**

The argument

**Heading**

You cannot improve what you refuse to see.

**Body**

The article draws on the idea of gemba kaizen: continuous improvement grounded in direct understanding of the place where value is created and problems actually occur.

Its central argument is simple:

When senior leaders become too removed from day-to-day operations, they are forced to understand problems through reports, summaries, and layers of management.

Something gets lost on the way up.

**Featured excerpt**

> Without a constantly updated understanding, in person, of what needs to be improved, senior management can only rely on the reports of their subordinates.

## The Numbers Change the Pace

**Section label**

From theory to evidence

**Heading**

$100 million to $400 million.

**Body**

To show what the idea looks like in practice, the article turns to Wiremold and the leadership of Art Byrne.

The results create a sharp break in the argument.

**Selected figures**

- Sales grew from $100 million to $400 million.
- Customer service improved from 50% to 98%.
- Enterprise value rose from $30 million to $770 million.
- Inventory turns increased from 3 to 18.
- Productivity improved by about 162%.

**Editorial transition**

Then comes the more revealing detail.

**Featured excerpt**

> All the companies that wanted to learn kaizen from Wiremold stopped attending the training sessions the moment their CEOs were mandated to attend.

**Large line**

> The people with the most to gain were the first to leave the room.

## The Distance Problem

**Section label**

Where improvement breaks down

**Heading**

The higher some leaders rise, the further they get from the work.

**Featured excerpt**

> In America, the higher company executives rise, the more removed they become from the nexus of company base activities, as this becomes the problem of subordinates.

**Body**

The article examines the logic behind that distance.

Senior leaders are expected to focus on the “bigger picture.” The details of the work are delegated downwards.

But there is a problem with a big picture built from second-hand information.

The people closest to the problems may soften them, simplify them, or decide they are too small to send upwards.

Meanwhile, the people with the authority to make fundamental changes see less and less of what needs changing.

## The First Question

**Section label**

Before the framework

**Large question**

> What are we trying to solve or improve?

**Body**

The article argues that many companies fail before the sophisticated problem-solving even begins.

If the problem is poorly understood, the framework built around it cannot rescue the work.

**Featured excerpt**

> Without a clear understanding and definition of the value chain that the company needs to improve, nothing can come out of any fancy planning.

## Four Things to Watch

**Section label**

Making the idea practical

**Heading**

Go and see.  
Then know what to look for.

**Introduction**

Drawing from the source material, the article identifies four areas leaders should keep close to:

### Customer service

What is the on-time delivery or response rate, and how can it improve?

### Productivity

How productive is the company per worker-hour, and what could increase it?

### Quality

Where are defects or unproductive services appearing, and how can they be reduced?

### Inventory turns

How efficiently is inventory moving, and what is slowing it down?

**Editorial note**

The article moves from argument to application.

The idea is not simply that leaders should be more visible.

They need to know what they are looking for when they arrive.

---

## The Wider Lesson

**Section label**

The bigger picture

**Featured excerpt**

> There is no big picture without a strong base.

**Body**

That line brings the argument back to its central tension.

Senior leadership is supposed to see the whole organisation.

But the article argues that the whole organisation becomes harder to understand when leaders stop seeing its smallest realities for themselves.

Continuous improvement depends on proximity.

Not once.

Continuously.

## Project Reflection

**Heading**

Making a business argument hold together

**Body**

This piece had more moving parts than a short promotional assignment.

It brought together:

- a management philosophy;
- a book chapter;
- a leadership interview;
- company performance figures;
- a problem-solving framework;
- examples from American and Japanese business.

The writing had to keep all of that moving in one direction.

The central question did the work:

What happens when the people responsible for improvement become disconnected from the place that needs improving?

Everything else supports the answer.
