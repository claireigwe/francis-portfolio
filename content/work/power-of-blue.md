## The Story in Three Moves

**Section label**

Building the argument

**Heading**

A triumph.  
The bad news.  
Then something better.

**Body**

The article uses a simple progression to move through a complicated subject.

First, it explains why synthetic dyes became so common.

Then it turns to their downsides.

Finally, it introduces the case for natural alternatives.

The section headings carry that progression.

### Move 01

**A synthetic triumph**

**Featured excerpt**

> To meet the upsurge in demand from a fast-growing global population, synthetic dyes have replaced natural dyes as the go-to option for fabrics because they are cheaper and more useful at an industrial scale.

### Move 02

**A synthetic bad news**

**Featured excerpt**

> Synthetic dyes do not just affect you when you wear fabrics they were used on. They can cause significant damage to the environment too.

### Move 03

**The natural good news**

**Featured excerpt**

> You’ve read the bad news, so it only gets better now.

## Making the Technical Human

**Section label**

From subject to reader

**Heading**

The science matters.  
So does the reason to keep reading.

**Body**

Rather than beginning with definitions, the article begins with a question the reader might already have.

Only after establishing that connection does it ask:

**Featured question**

> What exactly is natural indigo dye, and how does it help your fabric?

**Featured excerpt**

> Natural indigo dye is a blue colour-yielding extract, mostly obtained from varieties of the indigofera plant native to tropical regions.

**Body**

The writing moves between explanation and consequence.

What is it?  
Why is it different?  
Why should the reader care?

That rhythm helps keep the subject from becoming a list of technical facts.

## A Detail That Sticks

**Section label**

History as a hook

**Large line**

> Among the Japanese samurai, natural indigo-dyed fabrics were a favourite.

**Body**

A memorable detail can give a long article somewhere to breathe.

The original piece uses history to make the subject more vivid and to move the reader away from abstract material science for a moment.

It is the kind of detail that makes an explanation easier to remember.

## Connecting the Story to the Brand

**Section label**

From education to product

**Featured excerpt**

> The case for going natural has never been stronger.

**Body**

The article spends most of its time building the subject before bringing the brand into the story.

Only at the end does it connect the argument to Aizome Bedding and its organic bedding products.

**Closing excerpt**

> Aizome Bedding has you covered on this. Our luxury organic bedding is made with natural indigo and organic cotton.

**Editorial note**

The product arrives as the conclusion to the subject rather than interrupting it.

---

## Project Reflection

**Heading**

Explain first. Sell second.

**Body**

Long-form brand content has to do more than contain information.

The reader needs a reason to enter the subject, a clear path through it, and enough variation to keep going.

This piece uses:

- an everyday opening;
- a clear problem-and-alternative structure;
- questions to guide the explanation;
- memorable historical detail;
- a final connection to the product.

The brand matters.

But the story earns the reader’s attention first.
