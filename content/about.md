## The Editing

**Section label**

Then the work widened

**Heading**

Writing taught me how to make choices.

Editing taught me how to question them.

**Body**

I moved into work that put me on the other side of the page.

Instead of only asking, “What should I write?” I was also asking:

Why does this section drag?

Where did the idea get lost?

Does this sound like the person or brand it is supposed to sound like?

What can disappear without losing anything?

What is the writer trying to say — and is that what the reader will actually understand?

That work sharpened the way I approach my own writing too.

## Content Compliance

**Section label**

Words also have consequences

**Heading**

Good content should not only sound right.

It should stand up to scrutiny.

**Body**

My work in content compliance added another layer to how I think about communication.

I’ve reviewed digital, marketing, and technical content, worked with teams across different functions, identified content risks, and helped create clearer guidelines for the people producing the work.

That experience made me more attentive to the things good writing can easily overlook:

What is this claiming?

Can the claim be supported?

Is the message clear?

Could it mislead?

Does the content meet the standard expected of it?

The best sentence in the room is not much use if it creates the wrong problem.

## Content Notes

- The About page should tell a professional story, not reproduce Francis’s CV.
- The narrative order is intentional: writing, editing, quality control and training, then content compliance.
- The page should show how each stage changed or strengthened the way Francis works with words.
- Keep the tone in first person throughout.
- The writing should feel warm and self-aware without becoming overly casual.
- Do not add a childhood story, “I have always loved writing” narrative, or other personal origin story unless Francis genuinely provides one.
- Do not invent employers, dates, responsibilities, achievements, or career transitions.
- The experience descriptions are based on the available CV evidence and should be checked against the CV again before final publication.
- The service list should remain secondary to the story. Do not turn the About page into a services page.
- The education section should remain concise.
- Do not add skill percentages, proficiency bars, or years-of-experience counters.
- Do not add a downloadable CV unless we deliberately decide it would help the portfolio.
- The exact visual composition will be defined during the visual direction and storyboard phases.
