# Design Rules

## Purpose

These rules govern visual decisions while building Francis Igwe’s portfolio.

For full creative direction, page treatments, and project-specific art direction, read:

`.agents/context/visual-direction.md`

For design values, use:

- `tokens/colors.css`
- `tokens/typography.css`
- `tokens/spacing.css`
- `tokens/motion.css`

Do not duplicate those sources here.

---

## Before Designing

Before changing a page:

1. Read the relevant content file.
2. Read the relevant section of `visual-direction.md`.
3. Inspect established site behaviour.
4. Use existing tokens before adding local values.

Do not design from the task prompt alone.

Do not replace established decisions with generic portfolio patterns.

---

## Core Principle

> The website stays composed. The words are allowed to misbehave.

The interface should remain controlled, sophisticated, warm, and readable.

Selected words and images may become expressive when the content earns it.

Expression must help the visitor notice, understand, read, or feel the writing differently.

Do not create spectacle without purpose.

---

## Overall Character

The portfolio should feel like a literary publication meeting a sharp modern campaign portfolio.

It should feel:

- witty;
- sophisticated;
- warm;
- editorial;
- contemporary.

It should not feel like:

- a generic dark portfolio;
- a developer portfolio;
- a tech product;
- an agency template;
- a newspaper replica;
- a scrapbook;
- a brutalist experiment.

If a design decision could belong unchanged in hundreds of portfolios, reconsider it.

---

## Colour

The portfolio is dark-first.

Do not create:

- a theme toggle;
- a second light theme;
- mechanical dark-light section alternation.

Warm Paper is an editorial surface, not a separate theme.

Use Editor’s Red deliberately.

Do not apply it to every:

- link;
- heading;
- hover;
- section;
- decorative detail.

> Most of the site whispers in ink and paper. Red appears when something deserves a mark.

Projects may temporarily borrow an approved colour atmosphere.

Do not give every project a random brand colour.

Follow `visual-direction.md` for project-specific colour behaviour.

---

## Typography

Use:

- Instrument Serif for display and expressive editorial moments;
- Geist for body copy, interface, metadata, and sustained reading.

Do not add another core typeface.

Choose typography according to meaning and function, not HTML level alone.

> The interface speaks quietly. The writing decides when to raise its voice.

Scale follows meaning.

Use oversized typography for genuinely important:

- lines;
- transitions;
- punchlines;
- quotations;
- phrases.

Do not make every page opening or section heading enormous.

The larger the type, the more important the line should be.

---

## Reading

Protect long-form readability.

Maintain:

- comfortable line length;
- sufficient line height;
- clear paragraph rhythm;
- strong contrast;
- sensible text size.

Do not stretch long paragraphs across wide screens.

Do not place sustained reading text over busy imagery.

Do not sacrifice reading clarity for atmosphere.

---

## Layout

Use the grid as an underlying discipline, not a repeated visible template.

Do not force every section into:

- equal columns;
- identical splits;
- identical alignment;
- identical dimensions.

Keep composition width and reading width distinct.

Large images and statements may use wide space.

Long-form copy should remain narrow.

Use asymmetry only when it creates:

- emphasis;
- rhythm;
- meaning;
- a useful relationship.

Do not move elements randomly to make a page feel editorial.

---

## Rhythm and Whitespace

Pages should feel edited, not assembled.

Vary rhythm through:

- quiet openings;
- large imagery;
- narrow reading passages;
- oversized excerpts;
- image sequences;
- annotations;
- full-width moments;
- quiet conclusions.

Do not stack identical sections.

Do not give every section the same spacing.

Whitespace should separate ideas, create anticipation, or give important moments room to land.

Do not create large empty areas merely to look premium.

Whitespace must have a job.

---

## Cards and Containers

Cards are not the default layout unit.

Do not automatically turn:

- projects;
- capabilities;
- experience;
- quotations;
- article sections;
- contact information;

into cards.

Use very little border radius.

Do not place every image or text block inside a rounded rectangle.

Prefer open composition, sharp edges, and direct relationships between text and image.

Avoid:

- card-heavy layouts;
- heavy shadows;
- decorative gradients;
- glowing backgrounds;
- blurred colour orbs;
- glassmorphism.

Do not use visual effects as a substitute for composition.

---

## Imagery

Every project needs a deliberate image world.

No substantial project page should become a wall of text.

Every image should answer:

> What does this help the visitor understand or feel about the writing?

Use imagery for:

- context;
- atmosphere;
- process;
- texture;
- evidence;
- emotional tone.

Do not add images merely to fill space.

Avoid generic stock imagery.

Follow `visual-direction.md` for each project’s approved image world.

---

## Image Treatment

Vary image treatment according to the story.

Possible treatments include:

- edge-to-edge crops;
- narrow crops;
- oversized details;
- image sequences;
- full-bleed moments;
- images interrupting the grid.

Do not place every image inside:

- a card;
- a browser frame;
- a device mockup;
- a rounded container.

Do not use one image ratio or layout throughout the site.

Do not repeat one image treatment across every project.

---

## Portraits

Use only real photography of Francis.

Do not use an AI-generated portrait of him.

Do not force a portrait into the homepage hero.

When suitable photography exists, use it as part of the narrative rather than as a profile badge.

---

## Editorial Details

Use editing-inspired marks sparingly.

Possible marks include:

- underlines;
- circles;
- brackets;
- strikethroughs;
- margin marks;
- short arrows.

> One meaningful mark is better than ten decorative ones.

Do not turn the portfolio into a literal red-pen theme.

Do not add fake annotations or marks merely for decoration.

Annotations must add context, rhythm, personality, or a useful second voice.

---

## Expressive Typography

Selected words or short statements may:

- enter margins;
- cross columns;
- extend beyond a boundary;
- disappear slightly beyond the viewport.

Use this only for non-essential expressive moments.

Do not crop:

- navigation;
- body copy;
- project context;
- essential headings;
- calls to action.

Remove expressive cropping on smaller screens when it harms clarity.

---

## Texture

Use texture subtly.

Acceptable treatments include:

- fine paper grain;
- extremely subtle noise;
- slight print texture where relevant.

Texture should be felt more than noticed.

Avoid:

- heavy grain;
- torn paper;
- tape;
- coffee stains;
- crumpled paper;
- notebook effects;
- scrapbook collage.

---

## Icons and Actions

Use icons only when they improve interaction or understanding.

Do not use:

- icons beside every capability;
- writing-themed icons;
- category icons;
- decorative emoji;
- icon-filled cards.

> If the words already make it clear, the icon can stay home.

Do not make every link look like an app button.

Avoid excessive:

- pill buttons;
- gradient buttons;
- glowing buttons;
- oversized rounded calls to action.

---

## Project Previews

Do not use one repeated project-card treatment.

Previews may differ in:

- image scale;
- image ratio;
- text position;
- excerpt visibility;
- interaction;
- visual weight.

Variation must come from the project’s content and image world.

Do not create random differences merely to avoid repetition.

---

## Project Pages

Do not create six visually identical project pages.

They may share a loose narrative structure, but each body must respond to:

- the writing;
- the subject;
- the imagery;
- the pacing;
- the project atmosphere.

Do not force visual uniformity for easier implementation.

Follow `visual-direction.md` for project-specific direction.

The portfolio provides the grammar.

Each project gets its own vocabulary.

---

## Motion

Motion should:

- reveal;
- pace;
- connect.

Do not use it to perform.

Do not:

- animate every section;
- animate every paragraph;
- animate every word;
- apply one reveal everywhere;
- make every project move identically.

Major editorial moments may move more slowly.

Interface feedback should remain quick.

Do not hide essential information behind hover.

Provide appropriate behaviour for touch devices.

---

## Responsive Design

Mobile is not a compressed desktop layout.

On smaller screens:

- simplify asymmetry;
- move annotations closer to related content;
- scale oversized typography intelligently;
- stack image sequences when necessary;
- remove harmful cropping;
- preserve the primary reading path.

Do not preserve desktop experimentation at the expense of clarity.

> When editorial experimentation conflicts with clarity, clarity wins.

---

## Design Review

Before considering a page complete, check:

1. Does the writing lead the composition?
2. Does the page feel specific to Francis?
3. Could it belong unchanged to a generic portfolio?
4. Is it relying too heavily on cards or containers?
5. Does every major image have a purpose?
6. Is sustained reading comfortable?
7. Is the project distinct from the others?
8. Does it still belong to the same portfolio?
9. Has motion improved the experience?
10. Does mobile feel intentionally designed?
11. Is anything present merely because it is trendy?

If a design choice cannot be justified by the content, visual direction, or interaction need, remove it.

---

## Final Rule

Create coherence through:

- typography;
- palette;
- spacing;
- editorial restraint;
- interaction quality.

Create distinction through:

- content;
- imagery;
- composition;
- pacing;
- project atmosphere.

Do not flatten the work into a template.

Do not create difference through random styling.
