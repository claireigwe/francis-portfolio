# Visual Direction

## Purpose

This file defines the visual direction for Francis Igwe’s portfolio.

It is a creative and design reference for the website, not a page-by-page specification, wireframe, component inventory, or implementation guide.

Use it to preserve the intended visual character during design and build.

---

## Creative Direction

### The Writer’s Desk, After Dark

A sleek, contemporary editorial portfolio that feels like entering the working world of someone who takes words seriously but does not take himself too seriously.

The visual world should feel closer to:

> a beautifully designed literary publication meeting a sharp modern campaign portfolio

than:

> a standard personal portfolio website.

The portfolio must be:

- witty;
- sophisticated;
- warm;
- editorial;
- contemporary;
- visually expressive without becoming theatrical.

It must not feel like:

- a typical minimalist copywriter portfolio;
- a creative agency website;
- a newspaper replica;
- a corporate personal brand;
- a scrapbook;
- a brutalist experiment;
- a tech or AI product;
- a luxury fashion brand.

---

## Defining Principle

> The website stays composed. The words are allowed to misbehave.

The interface should remain controlled and sophisticated.

Inside that frame, words may:

- become oversized;
- interrupt the grid;
- move into the margin;
- occupy more space than an image;
- become a visual pause;
- appear as a quiet footnote;
- receive an editorial mark;
- take over a composition.

Every expressive treatment must help the visitor read, notice, understand, or feel the writing differently.

Do not use typography as spectacle without purpose.

---

## Visual Personality

### Editorial, not newspaper-like

Typography and composition should matter deeply.

Do not rely on:

- newspaper columns everywhere;
- issue numbers;
- excessive rules;
- publication clichés.

### Dark, not techy

The site is dark-first.

Do not use:

- neon gradients;
- glowing orbs;
- glassmorphism;
- futuristic AI aesthetics;
- blue-black developer-portfolio styling.

### Minimal, not empty

Use restraint without creating meaningless emptiness.

Whitespace should:

- separate ideas;
- create anticipation;
- give important moments room to land.

### Witty, not quirky

Personality should come from:

- writing;
- composition;
- timing;
- unexpected scale;
- editorial observation.

Do not rely on novelty interactions or decorative doodles.

### Premium, not luxurious

The site should feel carefully made.

It should not resemble a fashion house, perfume brand, or luxury editorial campaign.

# Typography

## Core Typeface Pairing

### Display

**Instrument Serif**

Use for:

- major narrative statements;
- selected page headings;
- large quotations;
- project-specific typographic moments;
- occasional expressive emphasis.

### Sans-serif

**Geist**

Use for:

- body copy;
- navigation;
- metadata;
- labels;
- buttons;
- supporting text;
- long-form reading.

---

## Typography Principle

> The interface speaks quietly. The writing decides when to raise its voice.

Do not mechanically assign:

- serif to every heading;
- sans-serif to everything else.

Choose the typeface according to the voice and function of the moment.

---

## Typographic Behaviour

Use strong contrast between:

- small editorial labels;
- calm page headings;
- oversized sentence fragments;
- comfortable reading text;
- quiet metadata.

The governing rule is:

> Scale follows meaning, not hierarchy alone.

A short sentence may be larger than a page title when it is the conceptual or emotional centre of the composition.

---

## Reading Text

Long-form reading must prioritise:

- comfortable line length;
- generous line height;
- strong contrast;
- clear paragraph spacing;
- readable type size.

Never sacrifice readability for editorial drama.

---

## Italics

Use Instrument Serif italics sparingly for:

- occasional human asides;
- emphasis;
- selected editorial moments.

Do not overuse them.

---

## Monospace

Do not add a monospace typeface to the core system.

Avoid:

- fake typewriter text;
- blinking cursors;
- typing animations everywhere;
- manuscript clichés;
- keyboard-key graphics.

The writer’s desk concept is contemporary, not nostalgic.

---

# Colour

## Core Palette

### Ink

`#11100F`

Primary dark background.

Warm near-black rather than pure black.

### Paper

`#F1EDE5`

Primary text on Ink.

Softer than pure white.

### Ash

`#A8A29A`

Secondary text and quiet information.

Use only where contrast remains accessible.

### Editor’s Red

`#D84A3A`

Primary accent.

Use for:

- selected emphasis;
- editorial marks;
- active states;
- hover feedback;
- occasional project numbering;
- small moments of visual interruption.

### Charcoal

`#1B1917`

Secondary dark surface.

Use for subtle separation, not card-heavy layouts.

### Warm Paper

`#E7E0D5`

Light editorial surface.

Use selectively for:

- reading-heavy sections;
- editorial inserts;
- project moments;
- major composition changes.

---

## Colour Principle

> Most of the site whispers in ink and paper. Red appears when something deserves a mark.

Editor’s Red must remain controlled.

If red appears everywhere, it loses meaning.

---

## Light and Dark Behaviour

The site has one intentional dark-first art direction.

Do not build a user-facing light/dark mode toggle.

Use Warm Paper and other light surfaces as compositional devices within the site.

Light sections should feel intentional, like opening a page, rather than following an alternating stripe pattern.

---

## Project Colour

Projects may temporarily introduce a colour atmosphere when the subject genuinely supports it.

Examples:

- Insatiable Eats may introduce a restrained food tone;
- The Power of Blue may allow deep material indigo to become dominant;
- Online Shop may use Editor’s Red more confidently.

Do not assign random colours merely to differentiate project cards.

The governing rule is:

> The portfolio owns the palette. Projects may borrow a mood.

---

# Imagery

## Core Principle

> Every project gets its own image world.

No project page should feel like a wall of text.

Typography leads the storytelling, but every project must contain meaningful visual moments.

Visitors should rarely move through more than one or two substantial text moments without encountering a meaningful visual change.

A visual change may be:

- a large image;
- an image sequence;
- a close crop;
- a figure;
- a diagram;
- a composition shift;
- a strong typographic specimen.

This is a rhythm principle, not a rigid formula.

---

## Image Selection Rule

Every image should answer:

> What does this help the visitor understand or feel about the writing?

Do not add imagery because a section feels empty.

Avoid generic stock photography.

Prefer images that provide:

- context;
- atmosphere;
- subject matter;
- process;
- texture;
- visual evidence;
- emotional tone.

---

## Image Treatment

Prefer:

- edge-to-edge crops;
- images interrupting the grid;
- narrow vertical crops;
- oversized details;
- image sequences;
- occasional full-bleed moments;
- image and text relationships with intentional tension.

Avoid placing every image inside:

- cards;
- browser windows;
- rounded rectangles;
- device mockups.

Use very little border radius.

Sharp edges and controlled geometry better support the editorial direction.

---

## Portraits

The finished portfolio should include at least one real photograph of Francis if suitable photography is available.

The About page is the primary location.

A portrait may also appear later on the homepage.

Prefer:

- editorial crops;
- off-centre composition;
- quiet full-width moments;
- multiple crops from the same photography.

Do not force a portrait into the hero.

Do not use an AI-generated portrait of Francis.

---

# Graphic Language

## Editorial Marks

Use small, controlled marks inspired by editing:

- underlines;
- circles;
- brackets;
- strikethroughs;
- margin marks;
- short arrows;
- emphasis lines.

Use Editor’s Red sparingly.

The governing rule is:

> One meaningful mark is better than ten decorative ones.

Do not turn the site into a literal red-pen theme.

---

## Rules and Dividers

Thin rules may structure:

- metadata;
- section transitions;
- excerpt boundaries;
- sources;
- navigation moments.

They should be structural, not decorative.

---

## Cropped Typography

Selected words or sentences may:

- extend beyond expected content boundaries;
- move into margins;
- cross columns;
- disappear slightly beyond the viewport.

Do not crop essential content in ways that damage readability or accessibility.

---

## Editorial Annotations

Small notes may behave like:

- margin comments;
- captions;
- observations;
- editorial interruptions.

Examples already present in the content include:

- “Three short beats.”
- “Then come the grandparents.”
- “A detail that sticks.”
- “Hear me out.”

Use them as part of the composition rather than treating every one as a standard section heading.

---

# Texture

The site should not feel perfectly flat or digitally sterile.

Use texture very subtly.

Possible treatments:

- fine paper grain on Warm Paper surfaces;
- extremely subtle noise on large Ink backgrounds;
- slightly imperfect editorial lines;
- occasional print texture within a relevant project.

Texture should be felt more than noticed.

Avoid:

- heavy film grain;
- torn paper;
- tape;
- coffee stains;
- crumpled paper;
- notebook effects;
- scrapbook collage.

---

# Iconography

Use icons only where they improve interaction or understanding.

Possible uses:

- menu controls;
- external links;
- email;
- LinkedIn;
- project navigation;
- directional cues.

Icons should be:

- simple;
- small;
- consistent;
- thin to medium weight;
- functional.

Use one established icon library.

Do not create a custom icon system.

---

## Arrows

Interface arrows must remain clean and consistent.

Editorial arrows may occasionally feel more human when they annotate content.

Never use hand-drawn or decorative arrows for critical navigation.

---

## Iconography Principle

> If the words already make it clear, the icon can stay home.

Do not use:

- icons beside every capability;
- writing-themed icons;
- project-category icons;
- icon-filled cards;
- decorative emoji.

---

# Layout and Composition

## Grid

Use a 12-column desktop grid as the underlying structure.

The grid should support varied compositions such as:

- 7 columns of text with 5 columns of imagery;
- 4 columns of context with 8 columns of primary content;
- 3 columns of annotation with 9 columns of excerpt;
- full-width statements and images.

The grid is a discipline, not a visible template.

---

## Width

Use a generous desktop composition width in roughly the 1400–1500px range.

Maintain a much narrower reading width for long-form text.

Distinguish between:

- composition width;
- reading width.

Never stretch long paragraphs across wide screens.

---

## Asymmetry

Use asymmetry intentionally.

Good reasons to break alignment include:

- emphasis;
- rhythm;
- meaning;
- visual relationship.

Do not move elements around merely to appear creative.

The rule is:

> Break alignment only when the break creates emphasis, rhythm, or meaning.

---

## Whitespace

Whitespace should:

- separate ideas;
- create anticipation;
- give important moments room to land.

Avoid exaggerated empty sections designed only to look premium.

---

## Vertical Rhythm

Pages should not feel like stacks of identical sections.

Vary the rhythm through:

- restrained openings;
- large images;
- narrow reading sections;
- oversized excerpts;
- image sequences;
- annotations;
- full-width visual moments;
- quieter conclusions.

Pages should feel edited, not assembled from reusable blocks.

---

## Full-Viewport Sections

Do not make every major section `100vh`.

Use full-screen moments only when arrival or emphasis genuinely benefits from them.

---

## Composition Tools

Use as needed:

- full-bleed image;
- split composition;
- image sequence;
- oversized detail;
- text over image;
- image with margin annotation;
- small image in large space.

These are tools, not templates.

---

## Text Behaviours

### Reading text

Controlled and comfortable.

### Featured excerpts

May expand beyond the reading column.

### Short lines

May become large compositions.

### Annotations

May occupy margins or sit beside related material.

### Metadata

Compact and quiet.

### Figures and lists

May break normal paragraph rhythm.

---

# Shared Page Structure

Individual project pages share only this loose skeleton:

1. arrival;
2. context;
3. body of the story;
4. reflection;
5. next project.

Do not create one rigid project-page template.

The body of each project must respond to:

- its writing;
- its image world;
- its pacing;
- its subject.

# Motion

## Core Principle

> Reveal the work. Pace the reading. Connect the moments. Then get out of the way.

Motion should:

- reveal;
- pace;
- connect.

It should not perform for its own sake.

---

## Motion Character

### Editorial motion

Slower and more noticeable.

Use for:

- major arrivals;
- large images;
- quotations;
- transitions.

### Interface motion

Faster and quieter.

Use for:

- navigation;
- links;
- buttons;
- hover feedback.

Do not use one universal motion duration.

---

## Page Arrival

Keep arrival quick.

Possible sequence:

- page appears;
- main headline enters subtly;
- supporting content follows with a short stagger;
- major imagery reveals separately.

Avoid:

- loading sequences;
- logo animations;
- counters;
- enter screens;
- repeated page intros.

---

## Text Motion

Animate selectively.

Possible units:

- line;
- phrase;
- occasional word.

Do not animate normal paragraphs word by word.

The rule is:

> Animate the unit the reader is meant to feel.

---

## Scroll Motion

Good uses:

- image reveal;
- featured quotation entrance;
- figure sequence;
- delayed annotation;
- progressive image sequence.

Avoid:

- every paragraph fading in;
- excessive parallax;
- long pinned sections;
- scroll-jacking;
- horizontal scrolling for ordinary content.

---

## Image Motion

Use selectively:

### Reveal

Simple mask or clip entrance.

### Drift

Very subtle movement within a large crop.

### Sequence

Related images reveal progressively.

Do not apply every behaviour to every image.

---

## Editorial Mark Motion

A mark may occasionally animate as if applied:

- underline draws;
- rule extends;
- circle completes;
- margin mark appears.

Use no more than one prominent animated editorial mark within a major composition.

Do not simulate slow handwriting.

---

## Project Preview Interaction

Project previews may:

- reveal a second crop;
- shift imagery;
- introduce project colour;
- expose a short excerpt;
- move a directional arrow;
- change the image-text relationship.

Do not use the exact same hover treatment for every project.

Essential information must never depend on hover.

---

## Project Motion Rhythm

### Insatiable Eats

Warm and flowing.

### The Power of Blue

Immersive and process-led.

### Gemba Walk

Precise and restrained.

### Online Shop

Quicker and more playful.

### Autovest

Sequential and pause-aware.

### Short & Sharp

Most experimental, but still controlled.

The rule is:

> One motion language. Different speaking rhythms.

---

## Navigation Motion

Keep desktop navigation quiet.

Use:

- subtle colour change;
- small Editor’s Red mark;
- extending underline;
- slight arrow movement.

Mobile navigation may use a full-screen Ink treatment with large typographic links.

Keep opening and closing quick.

---

## Cursor

Do not use a permanent custom cursor.

A small contextual treatment may be considered later for project previews only if it remains clear and responsive.

---

## Reduced Motion

Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`.

When reduced motion is requested:

- remove non-essential movement;
- remove drift and parallax;
- make reveals immediate or nearly immediate;
- preserve all content and functionality.

Build this behaviour from the beginning.

---

# Responsive Composition

Mobile must not be a compressed desktop layout.

On smaller screens:

- simplify asymmetry;
- move annotations closer to related content;
- scale oversized typography intelligently;
- stack image sequences when needed;
- never crop text for effect;
- provide touch equivalents for hover interactions;
- prioritise reading clarity.

The mobile experience should remain art-directed.

When editorial experimentation conflicts with clarity, clarity wins.

---

# Final Visual Direction Principles

1. The website stays composed. The words are allowed to misbehave.
2. The interface speaks quietly. The writing decides when to raise its voice.
3. Scale follows meaning, not hierarchy alone.
4. Most of the site whispers in ink and paper. Red appears when something deserves a mark.
5. The portfolio owns the palette. Projects may borrow a mood.
6. Every project gets its own image world.
7. No image enters merely because a section feels empty.
8. One meaningful editorial mark is better than ten decorative ones.
9. A disciplined grid sits underneath an edited rhythm.
10. The portfolio provides the grammar. Each project gets its own vocabulary.
11. One motion language supports different speaking rhythms.
12. Reveal the work, pace the reading, connect the moments, then get out of the way.
